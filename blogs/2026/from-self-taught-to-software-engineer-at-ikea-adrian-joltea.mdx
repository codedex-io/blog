Welcome back to our newest series: Codédex Learner Stories! 📕

For **Learner Story #004**, we're featuring one of our superusers, Adrian from Romania (you might know him as Lich King in our Discord)! He landed a Software Engineer role at IKEA!!!

**As someone who didn’t attend college or a bootcamp**, this is a HUGE win. I remember seeing Adrian in voice chats on Friday nights, working on his portfolio, so this is beyond deserved.

Congrats, Adrian. Proud of you. 🥹👏👏

## Adrian! Tell us a little bit about yourself.

Hi, I’m [Adrian Joltea](https://codedex.io/@adrian7), a **Software Engineer** at IKEA and I'm based in Romania.

My journey into tech started in 2023. I didn’t come from a traditional CS background, and I was learning by myself online. But after a few months, I had to face a hard truth: I wasn’t really progressing. I was consuming content, but not building... I was stuck in tutorial hell.

And that realization pushed me to reset. 

Around June 2023, I discovered Codédex. That’s when things shifted. I decided to focus on frontend development. In the beginning, I asked extremely basic questions, things that now feel obvious. But at that time, they weren’t. What made the difference wasn’t just the content... it was the community. People answered. They encouraged me. They didn’t judge. That gave me momentum. From there, I became consistent. I didn’t try to be impressive; I tried to improve. 

![What did Adrian do?](https://i.imgur.com/Po6YNUF.gif)
<Caption text="https://codedex.io/@adrian7" />

I learned daily, joined hackathons, built small projects, asked better questions, and slowly started understanding how real software is built. Over time, learning turned into execution. In August 2024, I received my first developer job. That moment changed everything. Not just financially, but mentally. It proved to me that consistency compounds. 

Shortly after, I moved to a new city – a personal and professional leap at the same time. Later, I joined IKEA as a Software Engineer, where I’m currently working. Being part of a large organization taught me a different scale of thinking: collaboration, systems, long-term impact.

Looking back, what started with uncertainty and beginner-level questions became a full career transformation in less than two years. The biggest lesson for me wasn’t just technical skill. It was this: Consistency beats intensity. Community accelerates growth. And asking “basic” questions is not weakness, it’s courage. I’m grateful for the people who helped me when I was just starting – and I hope to do the same for others now.

## What was your goal when you started learning?

My goal when I started learning to code was to change my direction professionally and move toward something that felt more aligned with who I am. I didn’t just want a new job – I wanted work that challenged me, allowed me to build, and gave me a sense of progress. Coding gave me that combination of structure and creativity, and I decided to commit to it fully.


## What were your biggest challenges?

One of my biggest challenges was navigating the job market while constantly questioning whether I was truly ready. As someone without a traditional computer science degree, imposter syndrome was very real. It’s easy to compare yourself to candidates with formal education and wonder if you’re missing something fundamental. There’s also the endless loop of “maybe I need to learn one more thing before applying.” In tech, there’s always more to learn, which can make you feel permanently unprepared.

Over time, I realized that companies don’t hire degrees – they hire problem solvers. I focused on building real projects, participating in hackathons, and proving my skills through execution rather than credentials. That mindset shift helped me move forward despite uncertainty.

## How did you overcome the imposter syndrome?

I overcame those challenges through consistency and perspective. I never seriously considered quitting – I just understood that growth takes time. When impostor syndrome showed up, especially because I didn’t have a computer science degree, I treated it as a signal to build more and improve further. Instead of waiting to feel ready, I focused on taking action. I worked on real projects, participated in hackathons, stayed active in the community, and applied even when I wasn’t 100% confident. Confidence didn’t come from motivation – it came from evidence. The more I built, the more undeniable my progress became. For me, it wasn’t about a breakthrough moment. It was about staying consistent long enough for results to catch up.


## And how did you stay motivated?

I stayed motivated by focusing on building real things and being part of the community. The biggest shift happened when I completed my first larger project after finishing several courses. For the first time, I wasn’t just following instructions – I was solving problems on my own and seeing everything come together. That project gave me proof that I was progressing. 

At the same time, becoming more active in the community played a huge role. Asking questions was important at the beginning, but eventually I started helping others too. That moment – when you can explain something to someone else – reinforces your own understanding!

## What advice would you give to a code newbie? 

If I could give one piece of advice to someone just starting out, it would be this: focus on consistency over intensity. You don’t need to know everything before you begin. You don’t need to feel ready. Start small, build regularly, and ask questions – even if they feel basic. Don’t get stuck in tutorial loops. Try to build something on your own as early as possible. That’s where real learning happens. Also, be part of a community. Learning alone is harder than it needs to be. Ask for help, and when you can, help others – it reinforces your growth more than you expect. Progress compounds if you stay consistent long enough.
