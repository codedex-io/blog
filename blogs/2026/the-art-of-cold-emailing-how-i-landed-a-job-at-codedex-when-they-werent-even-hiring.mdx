## No Prior Experience

üí°  The **"Experience Paradox"** is the vicious catch-22 in the job market where:

1. You need experience to get an internship.
2. You need an internship to get experience.

The classic meme in [r/csMajors](https://www.reddit.com/r/csmajors) is that even "entry-level" roles now ask for 2-3 years of experience and junior hiring has decreased by 22% due to AI and the broader downturn.

Breaking into tech is less about good grades (I didn't even list my GPA on the resume) and more about proving you can operate in real-world environments. This recruiting cycle, I was fortunate to receive multiple offers without any prior SWE internships. So how did I escape the paradox?

There are two easy ways to build resume value when you‚Äôre stuck in that loop.

### Undergrad Research as a Signal

The first is research. I started by lookinthg through my university‚Äôs [CS faculty pages](https://cse.osu.edu/directory/faculty) and reading about the work professors were doing. When something genuinely interested me, I reached out with a cold email referencing their research and explaining how I could contribute.

Research does two things well. First, it shows depth. Second, it signals that you can work on ambiguous, long-term problems. Whether it‚Äôs machine learning, computer vision, or systems, contributing meaningfully to a research project, especially one that leads to a publication or conference exposure, carries real weight in recruiting and graduate school admissions.

### Personal Projects That Actually Matter

The second is personal projects, which are almost non-negotiable in 2026 when applying to internships. A strong project answers a simple question: what problem does this solve, and why does it matter? You will most likely be asked about your projects in behavioral interviews, so it is important you actually know what you are building. Additionally, questions like ‚Äúwhat was one time you faced a challenge‚Äù can be answered using experiences from projects.

Good projects demonstrate end-to-end ownership. That means design decisions, tradeoffs, failures, iteration, and measurable outcomes. I focused on building projects that used technologies relevant to the roles I was applying for and that I could confidently explain at a whiteboard. Wherever possible, I included metrics: scale, performance improvements, user impact, or data size. Numbers make projects real.

## LeetCode: Boring But Necessary (Caveat)

There‚Äôs no way around it: [LeetCode](https://leetcode.com) still matters. At least in 2026.

I treated it more like a training session, kind of like how you might go to the gym to train your biceps. On average, I spent about 30-45 minutes a day, 4-5 days a week, consistently over several months. I focused less on raw problem count and more on pattern recognition: arrays, two pointers, stacks, trees, graphs, and dynamic programming. At the end of high school, I had around 150 problems solved. During college, I had solved a hundred or so more.

I tracked my mistakes, revisited weak areas, and practiced explaining my thought process out loud. **Interviews reward clarity more than speed.** My mentor's advice: "Always keep talking. Even when you are stuck." A large part of the technical is to gauge how you approach problems.

On days when I was really tired from school or life, I'd switch things up and go on Cod√©dex to make progress on [courses](https://codedex.io/courses) and [code challenges](https://www.codedex.io/challenges). No skipping leg days! üî•

**Note:** A common mistake students typically make is focusing on practicing LeetCode problems when they aren‚Äôt even passing the resume screen for job postings. LeetCode is necessary, but only when your resume is strong enough to get those technical interviews. At the start, focus on building your resume and personal projects ‚Äì later on is when you grind LeetCode.

## My Interview Process

For most reputable companies, the interview process starts with an **online assessment (OA)** that most commonly consists of [Data Structures & Algorithms](https://www.codedex.io/data-structures-and-algorithms) questions. For some companies, you need to score well on the OA for your resume to be considered. For others, the OA comes after your resume passed the screening and a good performance leads to an interview. 

Afterwards, you'll have to complete two types of interviews. The first type is **behavioral**, which evaluates your communication skills and workplace fit.

**Common questions asked are:**

- _Tell me about yourself._ üëà
- _Why do you want to work at [blank] company?_ üëà
- _Why software engineering? / Why CS?_
- _Tell me one challenge you faced and what you learned from it?_
- _Do you have any questions for me?_

My personal way to approach this interview is planning ahead for the first two questions since they are almost always asked. For the rest of the questions, I will have stories I can talk about for each of my experiences and projects that I can relate back to the goal of each question.

Not all companies will have **technical interviews**, but the majority of big tech will. This typically consists of a live 1:1 session with an engineer at the company, where you work through one or more problems in real time. These interviews are designed to evaluate how you break down ambiguous problems and communicate your thought process. 

Make sure to do a mock interview with a friend beforehand!

Out of 3 problems in my technical interview, I had only solved 2 but still passed. üòÖ
