I was doing [Daily Challenges](https://www.codedex.io/daily-challenge/) back in March and was getting frustrated because it had been a handful of daily problems since I got all the test cases right. It’s been two years since I coded on a regular basis, and even more since coding in a problem-solving capacity. Showing up for something consistently that gives me anxiety (challenge-style coding) is difficult, but it wasn’t always this way. If there’s been something in your life that you’ve felt like was your whole world - a hobby, a career path, a major, or a dream - but you don’t feel as connected to it anymore and don’t know what’s next, this blog post is for you.

# # Blueprint for the dream

When I was four, I visited a tech office in San Francisco for the first time. It was colorful, the food was bottomless, and they had every amenity you could think of. I didn’t know what getting to an office like that entailed, but I knew I was going to work somewhere like there. 

8th grade was the first time I got a taste of HTML (and coding altogether) and built websites about whatever we wanted, and I was the only one in my class who enjoyed it. My options were limited since I lived in the Philippines at the time, but I played with Scratch to learn fundamentals and make games, found apps to learn iOS development, and practiced JavaScript every day with daily lessons. Making stuff and solving puzzles was fun, and it felt like it was what I wanted to do for the long run. My moodboards on my walls were plastered with photos of dream tech companies in New York and while it felt far away, I was motivated to end up in one of those buildings.

![Early years](https://github.com/codedex-io/blog/blob/3d718626cc784553d118c99ae84755eaa91b7134/blog-images/its-ok-to-change/change-blog-post%201.png)

In 11th grade, I decided to double down with all things coding after moving to America. I was lucky to be able to attend college-level bootcamps and signed up for AP Computer Science at my high school. The summer after that, I participated in [Kode with Klossy](https://www.kodewithklossy.com/), taught kindergarteners how to program robots, and led tours about computer artifacts. I was also peer pressured to get LinkedIn…not proud of that one 💀 I got into college for Computer Science, picked the school out of my options that had cool tech clubs, and committed. I was knee deep in tech at the start of college. I signed up for a tech organization before I even got to campus, and was working at two startups. People in my year were asking me how I had so many jobs already. Upperclassmen were confused why I was so hyper focused on the industry so early. Looking back, so am I. 

I wanted to build the future my 13 year old self had been envisioning for forever, and I knew early on that I’d have to be prepared to even just get my foot in the door. My coursework was alright, and I was progressing at a pace I was happy with. That is, until I hit an infamous weed-out class: Data Structures and Algorithms.

![Locking in](https://github.com/codedex-io/blog/blob/3d718626cc784553d118c99ae84755eaa91b7134/blog-images/its-ok-to-change/change-blog-post%202.png)

# # When the dream feels like a chore

The only professor teaching DSA was tougher than steel. When I thought I understood what was going on, I’d do poorly on quizzes. Every project took over 20 hours, even when my friends and I chipped away at it together. Even when I felt I was doing fine, I ended up having to retake the class alongside other heavy ones. I didn’t understand a single bit of assembly, and discrete math gave me migraines every time I tried to read a proof. Even if how I felt was due to a disconnect in teaching styles, tough professors, and health problems - I’d still never felt so dumb. 

In 2022, I won an award for tech that ended up getting international recognition. People congratulated me all the time for weeks, and people said that they aspired to do what I was doing. In the background, I was failing and kicking myself because I thought I needed to work harder.

![Probably cooked](https://github.com/codedex-io/blog/blob/3d718626cc784553d118c99ae84755eaa91b7134/blog-images/its-ok-to-change/change-blog-post%203.png)

I spent late nights doomscrolling internship listings for 2 years straight just to get dropped after an interview or two. I hit a breaking point when I went from absolute clear skin, to eczema flaring to the point where it hurt to walk. My body was literally *begging* me to drop out of Computer Science. But I didn’t know any other world that wasn’t tech. 

# # The sunk cost Fallacy

> *The sunk cost fallacy is **the irrational tendency to continue an endeavor based on past investments of time, money, or effort, rather than future benefits**. It occurs because people want to avoid feeling wasteful or losing what they have already invested, leading to poor decisions in relationships, business, and daily life.*

There were a lot of reasons why I kept pushing, even when tech was a square peg in a round hole. Sometimes, it felt like a waste building up all these experiences, living and breathing tech, just to not even want to be close to it. I felt like I wasted resources and time in college learning things that didn’t really mean anything to me anymore past buzzwords and syntax. I hustled at jobs doing free work half the time, just to not be able to do anything with those experiences. I felt my parents would be disappointed in me. I made so many friends in tech and was scared of what they thought. I didn’t want people to think I wasn’t smart enough. I wanted to prove myself to everyone. But I realistically didn’t want to continue with the major, and it was making me miserable on all fronts. Being lost after committing to something for seven years felt paralyzing. 

# # The Hiatus (and discovering what was next)

So, I left college and took a year off to live at home. It was the first time I freed myself of any tech commitments in years. I got to just be a normal 20 year old for a bit, spending time with family, my cat, and my only worry being what kind of dinner I was craving. I got treatment for eczema. And funnily enough, after I stepped back, I got my first “big girl” internship. Weird how things sort themselves out.

![Hiatus](https://github.com/codedex-io/blog/blob/3d718626cc784553d118c99ae84755eaa91b7134/blog-images/its-ok-to-change/change-blog-post%204.png)

I still wanted to get a degree, but wasn’t sure what to switch to. There were so many paths I could’ve considered (that I’m sure a lot of you reading this post have, too). Product Management, to hone in on leadership skills. Teaching, maybe in a technical way, but there weren’t a lot of promising openings for that. Graphic design, but it didn’t pay as much as I’d like it to.

# # Restructuring the dream

One of my hobbies was graphic design, rooted in decorating my school slideshow presentations and crafting my high school yearbook’s layouts. 
There was another area closely related to graphic design: product design, which meant creating user experiences, researching, prototyping, and putting together the visual design of tech products. Product design was something I tried to get into, but had such a mix of resources that felt subjective and scattered. Since I was starting from scratch, it felt like the perfect area to dive into with a structured program. It ultimately meant leaving the college where my closest friends were, but it also meant starting something that would ultimately be a better fit for both my professional goals and my wellbeing.

It never fully occurred to me that there could be compromises to what I wanted from this industry. I could still code for fun with no constraints. 
I could combine technology and design in the workforce. I could integrate aspects of something I did for fun into my job. 
I could still work at a tech company if I wasn’t a straight up engineer. 
I could still live this dream, even if it wasn’t 100% what I envisioned it when I was too young to understand how hard it would be. 
Most importantly, I realized that **my contributions to this industry didn’t have to be my entire identity.** 

# # How I keep tech in my life now
Skills overlapped more than I expected, and even if I stepped away from the industry a good amount in the beginning, I kept coming back because my life required me to in some ways. This includes:
- Keeping in touch with my many friends in the tech industry
- Going to hackathons and conferences solely to meet the aforementioned friends!
- Trying to code projects that are fun, playful, and design oriented - instead of beefy classroom projects that drain my energy
- Keeping up with tech trends, even if it's in meme format, to make it feel a little lighter
- Joined hackNY, a nonprofit that promotes NYC startups to keep up with the startup scene and meet other technologists 

Coding still began to show up in my new major and throughout my job, and I didn't have to leave it behind. Sometimes, you don't always have to choose between two paths because one ends up intersecting with the other.

# # Closing Thoughts

Now I look at Daily Challenges, and the other technical aspects in my life, very differently. There’s no pressure from a system that didn’t work for me, and I’m choosing to grow my skills and keep learning how to code for myself and no one else. Safe to say, if you’re scared to try again, there’s lots of resources on here that’ll help you get to a place of understanding and growth. 

A lot of things have changed — my major, my school, my job titles, my goals. It’s easy to forget that change is naturally human, and it’s a gift to be able to have the choice to change your mind. No path is linear, no matter how much it’s advertised. Some people jump into tech after aspiring to become doctors. Some people work jobs totally different from Computer Science after majoring in it. It took me a lot of years to learn that I could change my mind at any point, and that those experiences and learning would still be valuable to me after I chose a different path.

There’s so much pressure nowadays to cram into certain boxes of “success” when success is entirely subjective, and such a fear of being perceived and judged for doing things that are good for *you*. I’m still embarrassed sometimes that I haven’t graduated, but always have people in my life reminding me that my circumstances were different and that I’ll figure everything out anyway. Whether you’re starting out in tech, or you’re phasing out — this is a gentle reminder it’s okay to change your mind. Whether your major, your career, or your passion for it, there’s no deadline to change how you feel about things in your life and try something new, even if it’s scary. 

Hope you enjoyed my lore. Don’t forget that it’s okay to choose another path if it’s not in tech, and that you’ll always have a community of technologists and a space to practice it if you choose! If you’re at a roadblock like I was and are comfortable sharing, I encourage you to share you share your story too, in the comments. We’re so proud of you!
